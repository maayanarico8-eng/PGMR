Fundamental Construction Rules:

Rule 1 - Construction Mode:
Every pictographic form in the language must be constructed exclusively through stroke-based geometry. Forms are defined by their boundaries rather than by filled areas. Filled regions are not part of the language.

Rule 2 - Geometric Vocabulary:
The language operates with a restricted geometric vocabulary. Every pictogram is constructed by combining simple geometric primitives into more complex forms rather than by drawing unique free-form shapes.

The language is based on a finite geometric vocabulary.
Current Primitive Vocabulary:
• Circle
• Ellipse
• Rectangle
• Square
• Line
• Arc
• Triangle

New pictograms are generated by combining and arranging existing geometric primitives rather than inventing new primitive forms.
All pictographic forms must be constructed exclusively through combinations and transformations of these primitives.

Rule 3 - Canonical Viewpoint:
Every pictographic representation must be constructed from a frontal viewpoint. The language does not permit viewpoint variation; all referents are represented frontally regardless of their category or context.
Rule 4 - Referential Consistency:
Once the pictographic representation of a referent has been established by the language, it must remain consistent across all occurrences. The same referent must not be reinterpreted, redesigned, or visually redefined according to context.
Rule 5 - Primitive Derivation:
The language does not invent new primitive forms. Every pictographic form must be generated by combining and transforming the existing geometric vocabulary. Complex forms are therefore derived rather than drawn.
Rule 6 - Primitive Manipulation:
Every complex pictographic form must be derived from the language's existing geometric vocabulary. New forms may be created only through the combination, intersection, resizing, and rotation of existing geometric primitives. All transformations must preserve the geometric identity of the original primitives. The language generates new forms by manipulating its existing alphabet, never by inventing new primitive shapes.

Permitted
(1 Combine
(2 Intersect
3) Proportionally scale
4) Rotate
Not permitted
(1 Arbitrary free-form deformation
2) Introduction of new primitive shapes


Style Specifications Rules:

Rule 1 - Stroke Weight:
Specification Stroke Weight 1pt
All pictographic elements must be rendered using a single uniform stroke weight. Stroke weight is fixed across the entire language and must remain identical for all pictograms.

Rule 2 - Stroke Color:
Specification Stroke Color #231F20
All pictographic elements must be rendered using a single uniform stroke color. This color is fixed across the entire language and must remain identical for all pictograms.
Rule 3 - Corner Geometry:
Specification Corner Geometry Corner Style: Rounded, Corner Radius: 4px, Radius scales proportionally with the primitive, Sharp corners: Not permitted.
All corners within the pictographic language must be rounded. Corner treatment is consistent across the entire system and must not be used to differentiate pictographic elements. The language employs a uniform corner geometry to maintain visual coherence. Corners are softened through a consistent radius rather than being left sharp or varying between pictograms. 
Rule 4 - Stroke Termination:
Specification Strok cap: Round.
All open stroke endings within the language must use the same termination style. Stroke termination must remain consistent across all pictographic elements and may not be varied to create different visual identities. The language employs a single stroke termination style to maintain visual consistency. Stroke endings are a stylistic property of the system rather than an expressive feature of individual pictograms.

Rule 5 - Stroke Joins:
Specification Strok Joins: Round.
All stroke intersections within the language must use a single, consistent join style. Stroke joins are a stylistic property of the language and must not vary between pictograms or individual elements. The language employs one uniform method for connecting strokes to maintain visual consistency. Join style must never be used to create hierarchy or differentiate forms.

Rule 6 - Standard Pictogram Size:
Specification Size: 64×64px.
All pictograms within the language are constructed and rendered at a single standard size. This size serves as the reference for all proportions, measurements, and stylistic specifications across the system. The standard size governs construction, rendering, and physical output. The geometry constructed at 64×64 pixels is rendered at 64×64 pixels without scaling, and the physical output reproduces this geometry without modification. The pictogram as constructed, as rendered in the interactive system, and as produced in the final physical artifact are identical.
